Background

What happened in Chicago?

In August 1968, tens of thousands of antiwar demonstrators descended on Chicago during the Democratic National Convention. Mayor Richard J. Daley deployed nearly 12,000 police officers. What followed — brutal clashes between officers and protesters in Grant Park, broadcast live on national television — shocked the country and defined a generation's relationship with the state.

The federal charges

In March 1969, the Nixon Justice Department charged eight activists under the Anti-Riot Act of 1968 with conspiracy and crossing state lines with intent to incite a riot. The defendants came from different factions of the left — pacifists, Yippies, and community organizers — united only by their opposition to the Vietnam War and the political establishment.

Legacy

The Chicago Seven trial became one of the most consequential political spectacles in American legal history. Judge Julius Hoffman's conduct — refusing to let the defense call witnesses, mispronouncing defendants' names, citing all seven for contempt — was so egregious that the Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals reversed all convictions in 1972.

The trial exposed deep fractures between a government willing to criminalize dissent and a generation demanding accountability over Vietnam. It galvanized the antiwar movement rather than silencing it. Tom Hayden later served in the California state legislature; Jerry Rubin became a Wall Street entrepreneur; Abbie Hoffman continued activist work until his death in 1989.

In 2020, Aaron Sorkin's film The Trial of the Chicago 7 brought the story to a new generation, earning widespread critical acclaim. The trial's core questions — about the right to protest, the neutrality of judges, and the weaponization of law against political opposition — remain as urgent as ever.
